# Objection Handlers

Scripts for the 6 most common objections from potential clients.

Rule: Don't argue. Understand. Then respond.

---

## Objection 1: "It's too expensive."

**What they usually mean:** "I don't see the value yet" or "I don't have budget right now."

**Script:**
"I hear you. Can I ask — too expensive compared to what? Is it a budget issue, or are you not sure it's worth that amount for your situation?"

*Wait for the answer. Then:*

If it's a budget issue:
"Would it help to split the payment into [X] installments? Or we could look at a smaller scope to fit what you have."

If it's a value issue:
"That makes sense — let me show you how other [freelancers/businesses] in a similar situation have used this. [1 specific example or result]. Does that help clarify the value?"

---

## Objection 2: "I need to think about it."

**What they usually mean:** They're not sold yet, or they need to discuss with someone else.

**Script:**
"Of course. Can I ask what you're weighing? Is there a specific concern, or is it more about timing?"

*If timing:* "When would you expect to be ready to move forward? I can hold the spot until [date]."

*If concern:* "What would need to be true for this to feel like a clear yes?"

---

## Objection 3: "We're doing this in-house."

**What they usually mean:** They want to save money or think they can do it themselves.

**Script:**
"That makes sense. Is the plan to hire someone, or have your existing team take it on?"

*If existing team:*
"One thing I've seen work well in that setup is using an outside perspective to set the framework, then your team runs with it. That way you get the best of both. Would that be useful?"

*If hiring:*
"Understood. If the hire doesn't work out or you want to move faster, I'm happy to be an option."

*Don't push. Leave the door open.*

---

## Objection 4: "We don't have budget right now."

**What they usually mean:** True constraint, or not a priority.

**Script:**
"Got it — when does budget typically open up? Q3? Q1 next year?"

*Get a specific timeframe.* Then:

"I'll follow up then. In the meantime, is there a smaller piece of this I could help with at a lower investment — just to get started?"

---

## Objection 5: "We've had bad experiences with freelancers before."

**What they usually mean:** They got burned. They're risk-averse now.

**Script:**
"That's fair — what went wrong? I want to make sure I understand what to avoid."

*Listen carefully. Then:*

"Here's how I handle that: [specific process or protection — e.g., milestone payments, a written brief, weekly updates, kill fee clause]. Would that address the concern?"

*Don't defend the previous freelancer. Don't oversell yourself. Describe your process.*

---

## Objection 6: "Can you do it for less?"

**What they usually mean:** They want the value but are testing whether you'll reduce your rate.

**Script:**
"I can adjust the scope to fit a lower budget — what's the number you're working with?"

*Then reduce scope, not rate.*

"For [lower amount], I can do [smaller version of the project]. We'd cut [specific deliverable or round of revisions]. Would that still give you what you need?"

*If they want the full scope at a lower rate:*
"My rate is what it is for this scope. What I can do is reduce what's included — but the rate stays the same."

---

## Objection Handling Principles

1. **Ask before you answer.** Most objections are questions in disguise.
2. **Reduce scope, not rate.** Discounting your rate trains clients to expect it.
3. **Don't apologize.** "I understand" is different from "I'm sorry."
4. **Know when to walk.** If they're haggling on a $500 project, imagine a $5,000 one.
