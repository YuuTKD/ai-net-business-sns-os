# Discovery Call Framework

A 20–30 minute structure to understand the client's situation before you pitch anything.

The goal: find out if this is a project you should take, and what they actually need.

---

## Before the Call (5 minutes)

Review:
- Their website
- Recent content or posts
- Any prior emails or messages between you

Prepare:
- Your calendar for availability
- 1–2 examples of your work that might be relevant
- This framework (open it during the call)

---

## Call Structure

### Opening (2 minutes)

"Thanks for making time. I thought we could spend about 20–30 minutes — you share what you're working on, I share a bit about how I work, and we figure out if there's a fit. Sound good?"

*Then: stop talking. Let them go first.*

---

### Understanding Their Situation (10 minutes)

Ask these questions. Don't ask all of them — pick 4–5 based on what they've already shared.

**Context questions:**
1. "What's the project you're trying to move forward right now?"
2. "How long have you been working on this?"
3. "What's the most important outcome you're trying to achieve in the next 90 days?"

**Problem questions:**
4. "What's getting in the way right now?"
5. "Have you worked with [your role] before? What happened?"
6. "What would a bad outcome look like for this project?"

**Priority questions:**
7. "Of everything you just described — what's the most important piece to get right?"
8. "What does success look like in 6 months?"

**Budget/timeline (only if not mentioned):**
9. "Do you have a timeline you're working toward?"
10. "Is there a budget range you've set aside for this?"
11. "Is this something you're looking to start soon, or are you still in planning mode?"

---

### Share Your Approach (5 minutes)

Don't pitch. Describe.

"Based on what you shared, here's how I typically approach something like this..."

Then:
1. Describe your process in 3–4 sentences
2. Mention one relevant result or past project (briefly)
3. Say what you would and wouldn't be responsible for

End with: "Does that resonate with what you're looking for?"

---

### Next Steps (3 minutes)

Option A (if it's a fit):
"I'd love to put together a proposal for you. I'll need [X from them]. Can I send it by [date]?"

Option B (if you need more info):
"I want to make sure I give you an accurate proposal. Could you send me [specific thing] and I'll follow up by [date]?"

Option C (if it's not a fit):
"Based on what you described, I don't think I'm the best fit for this — but I can point you toward someone who might be. Would that be helpful?"

---

### After the Call (within 24 hours)

Send a follow-up email:

```
Hi [Name],

Thanks for the call today. Here's what I heard:
- [1 sentence on their goal]
- [1 sentence on their challenge]
- [1 sentence on the timeline/budget]

I'll send a proposal by [date] covering [scope].

If anything changes or you have questions before then, just reply here.

— [Your name]
```

---

## Red Flags — When to Walk Away

- They can't describe what success looks like
- Budget is significantly below your minimum
- They've had 3+ previous freelancers "not work out"
- They want to own your process or tools
- The decision-maker isn't on the call and you can't get access to them

Trust your gut. A difficult client at the proposal stage becomes a nightmare at the invoice stage.
